Corporations Go Back To School: Colleges Attract Naming Rights Deals

 

CORPORATIONS GO BACK TO SCHOOL: COLLEGES ATTRACT NAMING RIGHTS DEALS

By Jeff Tanner,
NSLI Research Assistant

Naming rights deals are becoming increasingly popular for collegiate teams as university presidents broaden their approach to fund raising. Commercialization of venue names is a result of increasingly complex economics in sports. Universities are beginning to target the "deep pockets" of corporations to help generate revenue while these corporations are drawn by the glamour of high profile sports and the seemingly endless marketing possibilities of having its name associated with a facility.

Historically, collegiate facilities have been named after individuals who donate substantial amounts of money or were past icons at the school. But universities are beginning to sell naming rights to corporate sponsors throughout the college landscape. While in some instances naming rights revenue is used for general university projects and scholarships, funds are more commonly used to build or renovate a sports facility. In College Park, the University of Maryland completed a 25-year, $20 million naming rights deal with cable operator Comcast Corporation. The newly named Comcast Center, scheduled to open in the fall of 2002, had been on hold for more than a year while the university shopped for a naming rights sponsor. School officials have publicly referred to the deal as the largest "gift" in university history. They also hope to increase revenue by selling naming rights to the basketball court, advertising signage, and luxury seats.

Potential corporate sponsors identify the value of increased name recognition, brand development, publicity, and promoting company goodwill as reasons to purchase naming rights for a universities sports facility. Some universities have, however, found several challenges associated with selling a facilities? naming rights. In some instances, the school is no longer identified with the facility they wholly or partially funded, and often, the public assumes the university is endorsing the sponsor and its activities. The general public may not even know the business practices of the company whose name is on the facility.

Despite the financial upside, a university may not always be in favor of entering into a naming rights deal. Former University of Minnesota athletic department official Pat Forciea admits that the money available is hard to ignore, however, "too many of the lines that separate college athletics and professional athletics have been blurred." Minnesota agrees, as the Gophers will not be selling the naming rights to their on-campus basketball and hockey arenas. Forciea said it would be difficult to explain to the family of the late John Mariucci, the schools hockey coach from 1952-1966, "why we took the name off his hockey arena and instead sold it to ?NewIdea.com."

Attempting to insulate themselves from critics who feel that institutions of higher learning should avoid making deals for purely commercial purposes, some universities are combining corporate sponsorship with a more traditional facility name. One such example, Ohio State University, accepted a $12.5 million naming rights deal from the Value City Corporation for their new basketball arena. The new facility name is Value City Arena at the Jerome Schottenstein Center.

Naming rights have become an integral source of revenue for stadium and arena development. While many deals at the university level are made with local companies that cite civic pride, branding, and name recognition as primary benefits, the amount of money generated can represent a significant contribution to the overall cost of a project. However, are universities ignoring other needs that may represent a part of the institutions publicly acknowledged mission? Building first rate athletic facilities and attracting lucrative corporate sponsors are usually not listed among a universities mission statement.

An argument can be made that without the money they raise it would be difficult to excel in education and research, attract a diverse student body, or make a cultural and economic impact within the universities community. Virtually all newly proposed sports venues are considering corporate sponsorship. Will this proliferation diminish the value of naming rights deals? Based on deals recently entered into by Texas Tech, Ohio State, Fresno State, and Maryland the answer is no. These deals represent $82.5 million in revenue each university can count on for many years. Like it or not, advertising signage at stadiums and arenas is an economic reality of modern sports.
