Sports Facility Financing Cases

 

This report provides brief descriptions of the results of cases brought to contest the financing of professional sports facilities since 1985. The cases are listed in chronological order beginning in 1985. Note that the Webfinds listed after some cases provide a link to a freely available online version of the case.

N.Y.P.I.R.G.-Citzen's Alliance v. Buffalo, 496 N.Y.S.2d 208 (N.Y. Sup. Ct. 1985). The plaintiff associations contended that the Erie County Board of Elections and the Buffalo Common Council acted arbitrarily and capriciously in rejecting a stadium referendum petition. The plaintiffs sought to force the City of Buffalo to conduct a referendum and to preliminarily restrain the City from issuing bonds connected to the construction of a baseball stadium for the minor league Buffalo Bisons. The court ruled in favor of the defendants, holding that their actions were not arbitrary or capricious and that they did not abuse their discretion.

Kelly v. Marylanders for Sports Sanity, Inc., 530 A.2d 245 (Md. 1987). The Maryland legislature passed legislation that authorized the financing and construction of a baseball stadium for the Baltimore Orioles. The plaintiffs petitioned for a referendum to approve the legislation. The secretary of state rejected the petitions, but a trial court ordered the defendant to accept the petitions. The defendant then challenged the writ of mandamus granted by the trial court. The appellate court reversed, holding that the stadium bills constituted one law, none of the individual parts could go to a referendum, and thus, the secretary of state did not need to accept the petitions.

Rice v. Ashcroft, 831 S.W.2d 206 (Mo. Ct. App. 1991). Taxpayers sought declaratory and injunctive relief to stop the construction and financing of a domed stadium and the supporting facilities. The proposed stadium was intended to be used to attract a professional football team to St. Louis. The taxpayers' contention was that the city and county could not approve the financing and construction of the stadium without first submitting it to a vote. The court disagreed, affirming the circuit court's ruling in favor of the government. The court held that the Regional Convention and Sports Authority, which was already authorized by statute, would be constructing the stadium; therefore, no referendum was required. The court also held that any benefits from the project to private persons were incidental.

Libertarian Party v. State, 546 N.W.2d 424 (Wis. 1996). The Libertarian Party challenged the constitutionality of the Wisconsin Stadium Act. The Party argued that the Stadium Act provided tax exemptions to the Milwaukee Brewers and that the Act was an invalid private tax law. The court determined the tax exemptions in the Act applied to the district and not the team. The court also found that the Act clearly serves a public purpose.

{Webfind}

City of San Diego v. Rider, 55 Cal. Rptr. 2d 422 (Cal. Ct. App. 1996). The city entered a lease-back financing arrangement to fund improvements to a city-owned stadium, which was the home of the San Diego Chargers. The arrangement was to go into effect without a referendum. The city filed an action to validate the arrangement. The trial court granted the validation finding the arrangement constitutionally valid. The taxpayers then appealed the trial court's decision. The appeals court affirmed, holding that the arrangement would not create a prohibited debt on the part of the city.

Clean v. State, 928 P.2d 1054 (Wash. 1996). A non-profit corporation and taxpayers challenged the constitutionality of legislation that provided public financing for the construction of a publicly owned major league baseball stadium to be used by the Seattle Mariners. The court affirmed the superior court's order dismissing the challenges. The court held that the legislative act promoted the general public welfare and the legislature had no intent to donate public funds to a private enterprise.

Tamar 7600, Inc. v. Orange County, 686 So.2d 790 (Fla. Dist. Ct. App. 1997). Taxpayers challenged a one percent tourist tax assessed by the county. The tax was to pay for the construction of a professional baseball stadium. The trial court dismissed the complaint, but the appeals court reversed the ruling. The appeals court held that the taxpayers had a right to challenge the county's power to assess the tax. The stadium was to be used to attract an expansion Major League Baseball team, but the stadium was not built and Orlando did not acquire an expansion franchise.

Citizens for More Important Things v. King County, 932 P.2d 135 (Wash. 1997). Citizens challenged the use of public funds for construction costs for a baseball stadium to be used by the Seattle Mariners. The citizens argued that use of county funds, before the county had a binding commitment from a the Mariners, was not a valid public purpose. The superior court granted summary judgment in favor of the county and the Supreme Court of Washington upheld that decision. The court rejected the citizens' contentions because the Stadium Act had already been found valid, and thus, the preconstruction expenditures were authorized.

Poe v. Hillsborough County, 695 So.2d 672 (Fla. 1997). Taxpayers challenged a voter-approved bond ordinance levying a local government infrastructure surtax. The surtax would primarily be used to finance the construction of a new professional football stadium to be used by the Tampa Bay Buccaneers. The trial court found that the stadium would serve a public purpose, but it invalidated the bond because under the terms of the bond the football team would receive $2 million of revenue from non-football game events at the stadium. On appeal, the decision was reversed and the bond was validated.

{Webfind}

King County v. Taxpayers of King County, 949 P.2d 1260 (Wash. 1997). County taxpayers challenged legislation implementing bond ordinances to finance the construction of a new baseball stadium for the Seattle Mariners. The trial court entered declaratory judgment for the county validating the legislation. The Supreme Court of Washington affirmed the judgment. The court held that the legislation was constitutional and the county had met its burden of establishing the validity of the legislation.

Brower v. State, 969 P.2d 42 (Wash. 1998). Brower challenged legislation that would provide financing for a new Seattle Seahawks football stadium and a soccer stadium. Brower raised numerous constitutional challenges to the legislation. The trial court granted summary judgment in favor of the state. The Supreme Court of Washington affirmed holding that the legislation was constitutional.

{Webfind}

Allegheny Institute Taxpayers Coalition v. Allegheny Reg'l. Asset Dist., 727 A.2d 113 (Pa. 1999). Taxpayers coalition and local residents challenged a public funding plan for two new professional sports stadiums. The new stadiums would be serve as home for the Pittsburgh Pirates and the Pittsburgh Steelers. Plaintiffs alleged that the defendant did not have the statutory authority to implement the plan and had violated its fiduciary duty to the taxpayers. The trial court entered summary judgment in favor of the defendant. The Supreme Court of Pennsylvania affirmed, holding that the defendant was within its authority and had not violated any fiduciary duties.

{Webfind}

San Francisco Forty-Niners v. Nishioka, 89 Cal.Rptr.2d 388 (Cal. Ct. App. 1999). Citizens began a drive to repeal two voter-approved initiatives that would allow for the construction of a new football stadium for the San Francisco Forty-Niners. One initiative authorized the City to use lease financing to borrow money for the construction. The other initiative amended land use laws to allow for construction on the proposed site. The Team brought this action seeking a writ of mandate prohibiting the city director of elections from qualifying the citizens' initiative to be placed on the ballot. The trial court granted the writ and the appeals court affirmed on the grounds that the initiative petition contained false statements intended to misinform voters.

Ragsdale v. City of Memphis, 70 S.W.3d 56 (Tenn. Ct. App. 2001). Taxpayers brought an action against the city, county and the Memphis Grizzlies alleging that acts passed by the city and county to finance the construction of a sports arena for the Grizzlies were unconstitutional. The city and county created a sports authority. The sports authority was to issue bonds for the construction of the arena and the city and county agreed to contribute revenue to this effort. The taxpayers alleged this was an unconstitutional lending of credit to a private entity. The trial court found the financing unconstitutional, but the appeals court reversed. The appeals court held that the sports authority's issuance of bonds and the city and county's contributions were not unconstitutional lending of credit. The court also held that funding for the sports arena is a valid public purpose, and thus, the city and county actions qualified under the "public purpose" exception.

{Webfind}

Fair Play Missoula, Inc. v. City of Missoula, 52 P.3d 926 (Mont. 2002). A non-profit corporation organized by citizens filed for an injunction to stop the city and another non-profit corporation from constructing a new minor league baseball stadium. The defendants wanted to construct a baseball stadium in order to attract a minor league baseball team. The plaintiff contended that the agreement between the city and developer granted an exclusive franchise, which requires voter approval. The plaintiff believed the agreement was invalid because it lacked voter approval. The trial court granted summary judgment in favor of the defendants and the Supreme Court of Montana affirmed, holding that the agreement did not grant an exclusive franchise.

Cohen v. City of Philadelphia, 806 A.2d 905 (Pa. Commw. Ct. 2002). A city councilman challenged ordinances that provided partial financing for the construction of a new baseball and football stadium. The new stadiums would be home to the Philadelphia Phillies and Philadelphia Eagles. The councilman alleged the ordinances improperly delegated power, violated the Philadelphia Home Rule Charter and violated fiduciary duties owed to taxpayers. The court rejected the councilman's contentions. The court held that the Charter was not violated and there was no improper delegation of power.

Consumers Educ. & Protective Ass'n, Int'l, Inc. v. City of Philadelphia, 808 A.2d 266 (Pa. Commw. Ct. 2002). This case also involved the construction of new stadiums for the Philadelphia Phillies and Philadelphia Eagles. The plaintiffs filed an action seeking an injunction nullifying certain city ordinances that allowed the city to enter into leases for the financing of two new stadiums. The plaintiffs are public interest organizations and citizens. The plaintiffs claimed the ordinances violate the Philadelphia Home Rule Charter and the debt limitation in the Pennsylvania Constitution. The trial court rejected these contentions and denied the request for injunctive relief. The appeals court affirmed holding that the there was no debt incurred in the constitutional sense because only current revenue was used. The court also held that the Home Rule Charter was not violated because citizens had the chance to discuss the lack of funding sources.

Moschenross v. St. Louis County, 188 S.W.3d 13 (Mo. Ct. App. 2006). The plaintiff developers sued the Coalition Against Public Funding for Stadiums and the county seeking an injunction to stop a voter approved amendment to the county charter. Prior to the amendment, the county agreed to help finance bonds that were issued for the construction of a new stadium for the St. Louis Cardinals. The plaintiffs wanted a declaration stating that the amendment did not change the obligations of the county. The trial court granted judgment in favor of the plaintiffs. The appeals court affirmed holding that the amendment cannot effect the agreements already in place.
